## Resilience in Prehistoric Persistent Hunter-Gatherers, Northwest Kyushu

Persistent hunter-gatherers -- those who maintained foraging lifeways for centuries after the arrival of agriculture -- are often dismissed as marginal populations in ecological decline. This project tests that assumption using permanent biological markers of developmental stress (dental enamel hypoplasia and fluctuating dental asymmetry) in skeletal remains from six coastal shell midden sites in northwest Kyushu, Japan, where Jomon hunter-gatherers persisted for at least 700 years after Yayoi agricultural populations arrived in the region. The hypothesis was that developmental stress loads did not change over time. No statistically significant differences in hypoplasia rate or fluctuating asymmetry variance were found between pre-agricultural Jomon and persistent Yayoi-period Jomon hunter-gatherers, supporting the interpretation that these populations maintained population health and resilience across a major socio-ecological transition.
